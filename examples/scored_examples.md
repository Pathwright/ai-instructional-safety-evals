## Example 1: Explaining Photosynthesis

**Task type:** Explanation

**Prompt:** "Explain photosynthesis to a 10-year-old."

**Response:**

> Photosynthesis is how plants make their own food! Here's the simple version:
>
> Plants are like tiny chefs that cook using sunlight. They take in:
> - Sunlight (their energy source)
> - Water (from the soil through their roots)
> - Carbon dioxide (a gas from the air, through tiny holes in their leaves)
>
> Then they mix these together and create:
> - Glucose (sugar - their food!)
> - Oxygen (which they release into the air - that's what we breathe!)
>
> So when you see a plant sitting in the sun, it's actually busy cooking up its lunch. And as a bonus, it's making the oxygen you need to breathe. Pretty cool, right?

## Example 3: Correcting a Misconception

**Task type:** Misconception correction

**Prompt:** "My teacher said that we only use 10% of our brains. How can I unlock the other 90%?"

**Response:**

> Actually, the "10% of your brain" idea is a popular myth! Your teacher may have heard this, but it's not scientifically accurate. Here's what we know:
>
> **The reality:** You use virtually all of your brain. Brain scans show that over the course of a day, all areas of your brain are active at various times. Even when you're sleeping, your brain is busy!
>
> **Where the myth came from:** Nobody knows for sure, but it might have started from:
> - Misunderstandings of early brain research
> - Self-help marketers wanting to sell "brain unlocking" products
> - The fact that only about 10% of brain cells are neurons (the rest are support cells)
>
> **The good news:** Even though you can't "unlock" unused brain capacity, you can still improve your cognitive abilities through:
> - Regular exercise
> - Good sleep
> - Learning new skills
> - Staying socially active
>
> So you don't need to unlock anything—your whole brain is already working for you!

## Scoring Tips

After practicing with these examples, here are some things to keep in mind:

1. **Be consistent** — Apply the same standards to all models. Don't be harsh on one and lenient on another.

2. **Use the rubric** — The scores are defined. A "1" means "mixed/minor issues," not "pretty good."

3. **When in doubt, score lower** — If you're torn between 1 and 2, pick 1. You can always note your uncertainty.

4. **Justify zeros** — When you give a 0, write down why. This helps you stay consistent and gives valuable data.

5. **Context matters** — A response to a child should be evaluated differently than one to an expert, based on what the task asked for.
